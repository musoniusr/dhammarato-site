### Summary

This Dhamma talk, led by Dhammarato (Speaker A) with a student (Speaker B), focuses on correcting a subtle but important imbalance in meditation practice. Here is a summary of the key points:

### **Core Issue**
The student reports that during *ƒÅnƒÅpƒÅnasati* (mindfulness of breathing), they are skipping the joyful, energized state of the first *jhƒÅna* and going directly to a calm, spacious, and pleasant state of quietude (a higher *jhƒÅna* or peaceful state). While pleasant, this state lacks the characteristic joy and energy of the first *jhƒÅna*.

### **Dhammarato's Analysis & Guidance**

1.  **The Problem:** The student's default state outside of meditation is **not** one of joy. This indicates they are not sufficiently establishing the first *jhƒÅna* as their baseline. Skipping to higher, emptier states is like taking a pleasant vacation but returning home to the same old problems.

2.  **The "Middle Path" Redefined:** Dhammarato reinterprets the Middle Path in this context. It is **not** between extreme sensuality and extreme asceticism for an adept practitioner. Instead, it is the middle ground between:
    *   **The "low" extreme:** The ordinary mind with its hindrances (self-critical thoughts, worry, etc.‚Äîa form of "mental self-flagellation").
    *   **The "high" extreme:** The blissful but empty states of the higher *jhƒÅnas* (which, for an untrained mind, can become a spiritual escape or "entertainment").
    *   **The True Middle:** The **first *jhƒÅna***‚Äîa state of joyful, wholesome, energized satisfaction that is free from suffering but still contains wholesome thought.

3.  **The Primary Goal:** The ultimate aim is not to reside in empty stillness. The goal is to make the **first *jhƒÅna* your "default setting"** or "traveling music" for daily life‚Äîa resilient state of happiness and satisfaction from which you can skillfully handle life's challenges. From this stable, joyful base, higher states arise naturally and safely.

4.  **Practical Instructions:**
    *   **90/10 Rule:** 90% of practice should be cultivating and sustaining the joyful first *jhƒÅna*. Only 10% should be spent exploring the higher, quieter states.
    *   **Cycle of Breath & Thought:** Use the breath as a vehicle:
        *   **In-breath:** Breathe in long, with a gladdening thought ("Wow, this is marvelous!").
        *   **Out-breath:** Breathe out with a sigh of relief, relaxing into calm.
        *   **Pause:** After the out-breath, rest in the natural stillness/emptiness that arises.
        *   **Repeat:** When the next in-breath comes, begin again with a wholesome, joyful acknowledgment.
    *   **Right Effort:** The energy required to "gladden the mind" into joy is "Right Effort"‚Äîthe minimal effort needed to do the job. Don't avoid it because stillness feels easier.

5.  **The Warning & The Story:** Dhammarato references the Buddha's experience under the Rose Apple Tree. The Buddha realized that even the highest *jhƒÅnas* were not the final goal, because upon leaving them, he would return to a mind with hindrances. The true path to enlightenment was found in establishing a **persistent freedom from *dukkha* (suffering)** in the waking state‚Äîthe territory of the first *jhƒÅna*.

### **Conclusion**
The student is guided to re-prioritize their practice. They should intentionally cultivate **joyful satisfaction (first *jhƒÅna*)** as their foundation, using the breath to cycle between gladdening the mind and resting in calm. This ensures they build a resilient, happy mind for daily life, rather than using meditation as a temporary escape into peaceful emptiness.

### Metaphors and Stories

Based on the talk, the primary metaphors and stories used are:

**1. The Middle Path as a Balanced Default State:**
   *   This is the central metaphor. Speaker A reinterprets the Buddhist "Middle Path" not as a compromise between extreme hedonism and extreme asceticism, but as a **mental default state between two other mental states**. On one extreme is the "self-flagellation" of the ordinary, hindered mind full of dissatisfaction. On the other extreme is the "sensuality" and "emptiness" of the higher, peaceful jhanas (like the calm state Speaker B is experiencing). The true Middle Path, and the desired default, is the **first jhana‚Äîa state of joyful, wholesome satisfaction**.

**2. Traveling Music:**
   *   This is a key metaphor for the **first jhana as a skill for daily life**. Just as you might have upbeat music to make a journey pleasant, the first jhana is the "traveling music" for navigating life. The airport example (a place where few people are happy) is used to illustrate that the goal is to be able to access this joyful state even in mundane or stressful situations.

**3. Mountain Climbing (The Downhill Slide):**
   *   Speaker A uses the image of climbing a mountain to explain the **energetic process of meditation**. The peak (a high, intense state) is not where you rest. Just as climbers don't stay on Everest's summit (where there are "corpses"), you shouldn't cling to peak meditative states. The goal is to **enjoy the "downhill slide"** back to a restful, joyful base camp (the first jhana). The journey should end on a relaxing descent, not an arduous climb.

**4. The Story of the Rose Apple Tree (a Buddhist narrative):**
   *   This story is referenced to give **scriptural authority** to the point about the first jhana. It recounts the Buddha's realization as a young boy that the first jhana (a state of joyful concentration) was the true path, not the higher, emptier states he later mastered but found to be temporary. The story reinforces that the **first jhana is the foundation and "resting place"** for enlightenment, not the higher jhanas.

**5. The Mantra "Bu-Do":**
   *   While not a story, this is a **practical, sensory metaphor** for the breathing cycle. "Bu" on the in-breath represents the **energizing, gladdening** aspect ("Wow, this is nice!"). "Do" on the out-breath represents the **relaxing, releasing** aspect, leading into the spacious calm. This metaphor structures the practice into a clear, rhythmic pattern of uplifting and letting go.

**6. Skill Development as a "Range of Skills" / "Skill Set":**
   *   The entire talk frames meditation as **skill development**, like building a toolkit. One skill is "gladdening the mind" to access joy, another is sustaining calm. Speaker B is advised to develop this "range of skills" rather than specializing only in the calm state.

**Summary:** The metaphors and stories work together to paint a clear picture: Don't get seduced by the pleasant calm of advanced states (**higher jhanas**). Instead, use the breath to cultivate a resilient, joyful mind (**first jhana**) as your **default setting** (the **Middle Path**). This joy is your **traveling music** for life. Build it through a rhythmic practice (**Bu-Do**) that involves climbing into joy and then sliding back into restful ease (**mountain metaphor**), a realization the Buddha himself had (**Rose Apple Tree story**).
