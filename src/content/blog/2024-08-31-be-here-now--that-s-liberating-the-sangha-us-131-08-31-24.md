### Summary

This Dhamma talk, led by Dhammarato (Speaker A), centers on two main themes: the practical use of **wholesome thoughts** and the liberating power of **"not sure."** Here is a summary of the key points:

### **1. On Wholesome Thoughts: Quality Over Quantity**
*   Wholesome thoughts are like a **toolkit or medicine**‚Äîyou don't need every possible tool, just a few reliable ones that work.
*   The goal is **repetition**, not variety. Examples given include mantras like: "Everything is all right," "Everything is fine," "Not a problem in the world," or "Wow, I can do this!"
*   The purpose of these thoughts is to **counteract and cure unwholesome thoughts**. If you try to catalog or judge every thought, you can get lost. It's better to have a few proven, uplifting thoughts to return to consistently.

### **2. The Liberating Principle of "Not Sure"**
*   This is presented as a **core, liberating teaching** (attributed to Ajahn Chah and Ajahn Po), in contrast to mere informational "Buddhism."
*   **"Not sure" frees you from the burden of opinions, doubt, and the need to know.** Society often pressures us to have answers, but most knowledge is non-essential.
*   It allows **flexibility and prevents suffering** when you are proven wrong. Clinging to being "sure" leads to rigidity and disappointment.
*   It is a direct antidote to unwholesome thoughts, which often arise from worrying about the future, regretting the past, or forming solid opinions about things we cannot truly know.

### **3. "Buddhism" vs. "The Teachings of the Buddha"**
*   A crucial distinction is made:
    *   **"Buddhism"** is vast, full of doctrines, opinions, and non-liberating information (like debates about rebirth). It is compared to **medicine that manages symptoms** but doesn't provide a cure.
    *   **"The Teachings of the Buddha"** are simple, direct, and **liberating**. They are the **cure for spiritual disease**. The core of this teaching is embodied in phrases like **"not sure," "be here now" (Tathagata)**, and **"empty" (Sunyata)**‚Äîpointing directly to present-moment awareness and release from mental fabrication.

### **4. Supporting Liberating Phrases**
*   **Tathagata ("be here now"):** Liberates you from being lost in the past or future.
*   **Sunyata ("empty" or "there's nothing to it"):** Reminds you that problems are often mental projections, not reality itself.
*   Together with **"not sure,"** these form a toolkit for **direct liberation**, moving beyond preparatory "medicine" to the actual cure.

### **5. Personal Testimonies**
*   Several participants (Santiago, Ivan, David) share how adopting this attitude of **"not sure"** and focusing on the present has reduced anxiety, increased flexibility, and improved their daily interactions, leading to a "winner's attitude."

### **Overall Message**
The path to freedom isn't about accumulating knowledge or a vast array of techniques. It's about **simplifying** and using a few direct, liberating insights to cut through doubt, opinion, and mental proliferation. The ultimate goal is to graduate from using "medicine" (wholesome thoughts) to experiencing the "cure": a mind that is present, open, and free because it is **"not sure."**

### **Core Metaphors**

    *   **Buddhism (in general) is like medicine** that helps you feel better or manage symptoms (e.g., many common practices, beliefs, and rituals).
