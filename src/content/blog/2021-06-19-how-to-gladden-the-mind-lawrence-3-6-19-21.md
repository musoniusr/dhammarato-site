### Summary

This Dhamma talk, led by Dhammarato (Speaker A) with a Sangha friend (Speaker B), focuses on using Buddhist practice to address a specific form of craving and dissatisfaction. Here is a summary of the key teachings:

### **Core Problem: "Being on the Make"**
The student describes feeling driven, agitated, and in a state of constant wanting, particularly regarding romantic/sexual desires ("girls"). Dhammarato identifies this as a classic manifestation of **Dukkha (suffering)**: the painful state of wanting something you don't have. He explains that this external searching‚Äîwhether for a person, a job, or status‚Äîstems from a deep-seated feeling of inner lack or emptiness, an attitude cultivated over many years that "I am not whole and need something external to fill me up."

### **The Buddha's Solution: Change Your Attitude**
The core teaching is that freedom comes from a fundamental **change in attitude**, from "I need her/I want her" to "She's nice, but I don't *need* her." This is the goal of the Noble Eightfold Path. The practical method to achieve this shift is through the correct practice of **ƒÄnƒÅpƒÅnasati (mindfulness of breathing)**.

### **Practical Meditation Instructions**
1.  **Recognize & Label Unwholesome Thoughts:** See thoughts of craving (for people, past, future) as "unwholesome" because they are about something **not real and present here/now**. They are imaginary and lead to feelings of deprivation.
2.  **Gently Remove Them:** You don't fight a crowd of desires at once. When a single craving thought arises (e.g., about a specific person), you acknowledge it and gently let it go: "Aha, I see you. Out you go." Dhammarato compares this to the Buddha saying, "I see you, MƒÅra" (the tempter).
3.  **Replace with Wholesome Thoughts:** Actively cultivate thoughts rooted in the present moment that generate feelings of safety, satisfaction, and well-being. Examples include:
    *   "Everything is okay right now."
    *   "I am safe and secure all by myself."
    *   "I don't need anything. I'm good to go."
    *   "Wow, I'm glad I don't have that [craving]."
4.  **Gladden the Mind:** This process of substitution "gladdens the mind," moving it from a state of suffering (**Dukkha**) to the cessation of suffering (**Nirodha**, the Third Noble Truth). You "walk and talk" yourself into feeling content.

### **Key Insights**
*   **You Can Change Your Mind:** The ability to shift attention (like when a door knocks) proves you can change your thoughts intentionally. You just need to remember to do it for your benefit.
*   **Craving is Cyclical:** Recognizing that the "on the make" feeling comes and go in cycles allows you to catch it early and choose not to engage.
*   **External Fulfillment Fails:** Looking for love or wholeness "in all the wrong places" (externally) is inherently unsatisfying. True satisfaction comes from within.
*   **The Present Moment is Key:** Wholesome, satisfying thoughts are always available in the **present reality**. Any thought about an absent person or situation cannot truly satisfy.

### **Overall Message**
The path out of restless craving and agitation is a trainable skill. By diligently using mindfulness to clear unwholesome, future/past-oriented thoughts and actively nourishing wholesome, present-moment thoughts, you can change your underlying attitude from one of lack to one of contentment and independence. This is the practical application of the Buddha's teachings to end suffering.

*   **Purpose:** To illustrate the root of suffering (dukkha) as a sense of deprivation. The speaker explains we mistakenly try to fill this hole with external things (a girl, a job, a guru) rather than addressing the internal attitude of lack.

*   **Description:** This is a direct reference to a popular song title, used as a story framework.
