### **Core Teaching: Two Kinds of Thought**
The Buddha categorizes thoughts into two classes:
1.  **Unwholesome thoughts:** Sensual desire, ill-will, and cruelty.
2.  **Wholesome thoughts:** Renunciation (not needing something to be happy), non-ill-will, and non-cruelty.

The practice involves recognizing and abandoning unwholesome thoughts by seeing their harmful consequences, while cultivating and sustaining wholesome thoughts that lead to peace and clarity.

### **Practical Method for Meditation & Life**
1.  **Recognize & Abandon Harmful Thoughts:** When an unwholesome thought arises, see it clearly and understand it leads to affliction for oneself and others, obstructs wisdom, and leads away from Nibbana. This wise reflection naturally allows the thought to subside.
2.  **Cultivate Wholesome Thoughts:** Deliberately generate thoughts of contentment, kindness, and non-cruelty. For example, think, "I am good right now; I don't need that to be happy." These thoughts are safe, cause no harm, and aid wisdom.
3.  **From Wholesome Thought to Stillness:** Once the mind is in a wholesome, satisfied state, excessive thinking can tire the mind. The next step is to **still the thought formation**‚Äîgently quiet the mind, like slowing down from walking to lying down‚Äîto enter a tranquil, concentrated state (the first jhƒÅna).
4.  **The "Cowherd" Simile:** The mind is like a herd of cows. First, diligently guide thoughts away from "crops" (unwholesome states) and into safe pasture (wholesome states). Once they are settled, you can simply "sit by the tree" and be mindfully at ease, checking in occasionally to ensure the wholesome state remains.
