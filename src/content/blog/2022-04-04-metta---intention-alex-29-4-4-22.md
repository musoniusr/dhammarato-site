### Summary

This Dhamma talk, led by Dhammarato (Speaker A) with a Sangha friend, Alex (Speaker B), revolves around the nature of **true Metta (loving-kindness) and the mechanics of intention and attitude in practice.** Here is a summary of the key points:

### **Core Teaching: Metta is an Outcome, Not a Technique**
*   Dhammarato clarifies that **Metta is the result of correct practice**, not a practice in itself. Framing it as a technique (e.g., repeating phrases like "may all beings be happy") can put the cart before the horse.
*   True Metta arises from **wholesome thoughts and intentions**. You can't genuinely wish happiness for others if you don't first cultivate happiness and goodwill within yourself. "You can't give people the gift you don't have."

### **The Speed of Intention and Attitude**
*   The talk explores how the mind operates at incredible speed through **instantaneous intentions, attitudes, or inclinations** (referred to as *Samma Sankappa* or right intention in the Pali canon).
*   Dhammarato uses the example of feeling a bodily urge and immediately moving to the bathroom without any internal verbal dialogue. This demonstrates that **action can arise from a fast, non-verbal intention**.
*   Real Metta is this **fast, underlying attitude of goodwill**, not the slower, discursive verbalization of it. Our habitual attitudes (e.g., victim mentality vs. winner mentality) are formed and expressed through these rapid mental moments.

### **Cultivating Wholesome Attitudes**
*   We are often conditioned from childhood into a **"victim's attitude"** (needing something external to feel okay), as opposed to a **"winner's attitude"** (where everything is a bonus).
*   We can change these deep-seated attitudes by using **slower, deliberate, wholesome thinking** to "talk ourselves into feeling good." This repeated cultivation gradually rewires the faster, automatic intentions.
*   An example given is how one greets a returning partner: with a needy, victim-minded "Where have you been? I missed you!" vs. a joyful, winner-minded "I'm glad to see you! I've been thinking about you."

### **Practical Advice on Practice**
*   **Catching the Wave:** The goal is to become aware of the very first intention or mental moment that starts a wave of unwholesome thought or frustration. Simply seeing it clearly ("Aha, I see that") can defuse its power.
*   **"Never Mind, Start Again":** Practice is not about constant, exhausting vigilance. It's about **waking up to *dukkha* (suffering/stress) when you notice it**, and then gently restarting your wholesome intention. Life must include relaxation; you can't be on guard all the time.
*   **Taking Breaks:** The discussion warns against "taking a break" by letting the mind fall back into unwholesome patterns. Instead, one should take a break from *striving* and let wholesome mind states ride naturally. If you fall back, the instruction is always: "never mind, start again."

### **On Learning from Multiple Teachers**
*   Dhammarato encourages exploring different teachers and perspectives, comparing it to having different teachers for different subjects like math or music.
*   He notes that having a solid foundational practice (like the one discussed) allows you to better integrate and understand other teachings without as much frustration.

### **Overall Message**
The talk emphasizes that genuine spiritual development comes from working with the **subtle, fast layer of intentions and attitudes**, not just surface-level techniques. By cultivating wholesome thoughts and a "winner's" attitude of appreciation, we naturally give rise to Metta. The practice is a gentle, forgiving process of repeatedly waking up and starting anew in the present moment.
