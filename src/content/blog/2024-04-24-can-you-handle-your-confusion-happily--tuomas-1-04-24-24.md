### Summary

This Dhamma talk, led by Dhammarato (Speaker A) in dialogue with a Sangha friend (Speaker B), centers on moving from **confusion and reactivity to clarity and happiness through practical investigation and attitude change.**

Here is a summary of the key points:

**1. The Problem: Ignorance in Feelings**
* All experience arises with one of three feelings: **liking, disliking, or confusion/uncertainty**.
* We are generally ignorant of these feelings. Crucially, we **dislike the feeling of confusion itself**, which creates a "thirst for knowledge" and perpetuates suffering.
* This ignorant reactivity is a learned habit, passed down culturally ("monkey see, monkey do").

**2. The Buddha's Discovery: The Middle Path**
* The Buddha found extremes‚Äîboth of intense meditative absorption (*jhana*) and severe austerity‚Äîto be unsatisfactory.
* He discovered the **Middle Path**, which is a "happy place," epitomized by the **first jhana**. This is a pleasure born from the mind itself, not from external sensual pursuits.
* When not on this path, we are in a state of "hindrances," which is likened to mental self-flagellation‚Äîbeing critical and unkind to ourselves.

**3. Embracing "Not Knowing"**
* A major cause of confusion is trying to know the unknowable (e.g., the origin of the universe, the precise results of karma, theological debates).
* The Buddha's approach is to **let go of these unanswerable questions** and instead **investigate present-moment experience**. Happiness comes from being okay with not knowing.

**4. From Faith to Investigation & Confidence**
* The Buddha did not teach **faith** (which is rooted in hope and delusion), but **investigation** (*vipassanƒÅ*) for **direct knowledge**.
* Repeated direct understanding builds **confidence** (*saddhƒÅ*), which is different from blind faith. It's the confidence that you can understand your mind and handle whatever arises.

**5. Wisdom with Feelings: Liking vs. Wanting**
* A key insight is to **separate liking from wanting**. You can like something (e.g., find someone attractive) without the ignorant, craving-based impulse to possess it ("I like it, but I don't want it").
* **Renunciation** as a stern vow often sets us up for failure and self-judgment. A wiser "renunciation" is the **vow to see clearly**, to observe our reactions with kindness rather than hatred.

**6. Practical Attitude Change: From Victim to "The Boss"**
* Our default, learned position is to be a **victim** of circumstances, reacting with unhappiness to life's inevitable problems (e.g., a spilled glass, a blocked toilet).
* The core practice is an **attitude shift**: realizing you are **"the boss" of your feelings**. You can choose your internal response.
* The goal is to **cultivate joy internally** and radiate it, rather than fruitlessly trying to "make all beings happy."
* The "**first knowledge**" is this very shift from a victim mentality to the confident understanding that you can handle anything and choose happiness.

**Conclusion: The Practice**
* The talk ends with an embodied example: the practitioner (Speaker B) notices how a previous reaction of contraction to a mess has been replaced with humor and ease.
* The instruction is to **practice this choice point in daily life**: see your reactive feelings (liking, disliking, confusion), investigate them without judgment, and consciously choose an attitude of "I can handle this happily."
