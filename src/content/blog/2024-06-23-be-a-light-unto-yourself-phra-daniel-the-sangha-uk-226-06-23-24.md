### Summary

This Dhamma talk, hosted by a Bhikkhuni (Speaker B) in Dhammarato's absence, focuses on practical approaches to **renunciation and dealing with unwholesome habits**, framed within the context of everyday practice. The key themes and advice from the discussion are summarized below:

### **Core Theme: Skillful Renunciation**
The talk emphasizes that renunciation is an **ordinary, gradual skill** to be developed moment-by-moment, not an all-or-nothing commitment. Trying to abandon habits abruptly ("0 to 100") often fails because habits are deeply ingrained.

---

### **Practical Strategies Discussed:**

1. **Using Wisdom First, Then Support:**
   *   Try to see the habit **with wisdom**‚Äîquestion the urge, see its dangers, and observe the underlying attitude (e.g., "Is this moment not good enough?").
   *   If wisdom isn't enough, use **external supports** without guilt. The host gives the example of a **time-locked box** for her phone/iPad as a "backup" to support renunciation.

2. **Working with Intense Urges & Restlessness:**
   *   For strong urges (e.g., masturbation, smoking) triggered by stress or restlessness, several methods were shared:
     *   **Focus on a Coarse Sensation:** Concentrating on the **tip of the tongue** can quickly calm a restless mind, making it easier to then apply wisdom.
     *   **Practice *While* Engaging:** If you can't stop the action, practice mindfulness **during** it. Observe the sensations and feelings without judgment, which can reduce the power of the habit.
     *   **Use Physical Practices:** Activities like **walking meditation, Qigong, or Tai Chi** can give the restless mind a constructive focus.

3. **The Central Role of Attitude:**
   *   **Avoid Self-Judgment:** The primary suffering often comes not from the action itself, but from **hating ourselves for doing it**. This judgment reinforces the cycle.
   *   **Cultivate a "Winner's Attitude":** Replace guilt with **self-kindness (Metta)** and positive reinforcement. Congratulate yourself for "waking up" to a lapse in mindfulness.
   *   **Apply the "Two Arrows" Teaching:** The first arrow is the initial mistake or lapse; the second, more painful arrow is our negative reaction to it. Practice not adding the second arrow.

4. **Sutta-Based Tools:**
   *   **MN 20 (The Removal of Distracting Thoughts):** The five steps for dealing with unwholesome thoughts were referenced:
        1. Shift to a wholesome object.
        2. See the danger in the thought.
        3. Simply ignore/forget it.
        4. Investigate the cause (like calming the body).
        5. Use determination (e.g., clenching teeth).
   *   **Similes of Danger:** Remembering similes like the **dog gnawing a bare bone** (getting no nourishment, only weariness) or being **dragged toward a pit of coals** can inspire dispassion toward harmful habits.

5. **On Mindfulness & Teachers:**
   *   **Mindfulness is Not Constant:** It's normal for mindfulness (sati) to come and go. The goal is to increase its frequency, not achieve 24/7 perfection.
   *   **Healthy Relationship with Teachers:** While teachers are valuable, ultimate reliance should be on **one's own experience and the Dhamma**. Teachers are human; the real refuge is the **awakened understanding within**. As the Buddha said, "Be an island unto yourself."

---

### **Overall Tone & Conclusion:**
The talk was supportive, practical, and community-oriented. The emphasis was on **kindness, patience, and skillful means** rather than harsh austerity. The participants shared their struggles openly, and the advice focused on **gradual training, using wisdom, and changing one's underlying attitude** from a self-critical "loser" to a compassionate "winner" on the path.

The session closed with appreciation for the shared wholesomeness and the supportive Sangha.
