The practice is to **"catch"** it and then respond with a glad, welcoming attitude‚Äî**"aha, I see you, Mara"** or **"cool, we caught you"**‚Äîinstead of criticism. This reframes failure as a successful moment of awareness.

**5. Baking in Piti (Joy) and Nurturing:** The practice of cultivating wholesome states is described using nurturing, culinary, and agricultural metaphors:
    *   **"Bake in that Piti"** ‚Äì likening the cultivation of joy to a process that requires consistent, gentle heat.
    *   **"Nurturing thoughts"** (Speaker B) ‚Äì treating positive thoughts like plants to be cared for.
    *   **"Nurture," "cultivate," "develop the skill"** ‚Äì these repeated terms frame practice as a gradual process of growth and habituation.

**6. The "Double Quality" / Run and Catch Your Breath:** Speaker B uses the analogy of a runner who alternates between **"pushing towards awesome"** (sprinting, applying effort) and **"catching your breath"** (easing off, accepting the current state as "okay, it's fine"). This story emphasizes the rhythmic, non-linear nature of practice, balancing striving with relaxation.

**7. The Game:** Speaker A explicitly states that **"making a game out of it makes a huge difference."** This frames the entire practice of mindfulness and thought management not as a grim discipline, but as a playful, engaging activity where "catching" thoughts and returning to the breath are winning moves.

**8. The "Bunch of the Wholesome" (referenced sutta):** While not elaborated in detail, Speaker A references a sutta that categorizes wholesome and unwholesome states like sorting items into a "bunch" or bundle, using a simple, tangible metaphor for mental qualities.

In summary, the talk uses metaphors of **navigation (compass, path), journey (base camp), play (game, swing), nurturing (baking, gardening), and friendly confrontation (catching Mara)** to make the abstract principles of the Eightfold Path and Jhana practice relatable, practical, and approachable.
