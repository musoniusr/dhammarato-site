### Summary

This Dhamma talk, led by Dhammarato (Speaker A) with Sangha friend Scott (Speaker B), centers on using everyday conflicts as opportunities for insight and spiritual growth. The summary is structured around core themes:

**1. The "Diaper" Mind: Ownership and Childish Conflict**
The talk begins by analyzing a gym incident where Scott got angry when a man demanded equipment he was using. Dhammarato reframes this as childish, "diaper"-level behavior: we only want something because someone else has it, and we falsely believe we own it. This pattern, learned in early childhood, continues into adult life and even global conflicts (e.g., Putin's actions). The wisdom is to see this dynamic and choose not to engage‚Äîto simply walk away and let the other have it, recognizing that nothing is truly "ours."

**2. "Not My Business": The Path to Peace**
A major practical teaching is adopting the attitude of **"Not My Business" (NMB)**. This means mentally letting go of things we cannot control and that do not truly belong to us‚Äîfrom gym equipment to geopolitics. By not taking ownership, we avoid the burden of protection, loss, and conflict. This is likened to being "poor in spirit" (not owning anything) as a path to peace ("heaven").

**3. Inner Friendship Before Outer Brotherhood**
The discussion contrasts approaches. The Christian ideal often starts with loving others outwardly, but Dhammarato argues this fails if you aren't first a friend to yourself. The Buddhist path starts **internally**: cleaning up your mind in the present moment, applying the Golden Rule to yourself (self-nurturing instead of self-criticism), and finding inner peace. Only from this stable inner foundation can genuine, non-conflictual community (Sangha) arise.

**4. Being Alive Now: The Antidote to Conceptual Thinking**
Dhammarato emphasizes appreciating the reality of being alive **right now**‚Äîthe breath as the ultimate gift‚Äîinstead of getting lost in conceptual "magical thinking." This includes:
*   Dismissing speculative doctrines like the **31 planes of existence** or **literal reincarnation** as irrelevant mental constructs. What matters is **rebirth in this moment**‚Äîwhether you are reborn into a "hell" of anger or a "heaven" of peace *now*.
*   Rejecting dwelling on the past (e.g., a childhood memory or a past "Jhana" experience) or an imagined future. **If it's not happening now, it's just a concept, not your direct experience.**

**5. First Jhana as a Present-Moment Litmus Test**
The talk defines the **First Jhana** not as a magical trance, but as a tangible, present-moment experience of:
*   Freedom from hindrances (like anger or desire).
*   Sustained wholesome thought.
*   A gladdened mind and a feeling of **victory** or success (like an athlete's triumph).
*   Crucially, it must be **attainable and verifiable now**. If someone claims a past "awakening" but is agitated in the present conversation, it proves the experience wasn't transformative. **True practice is about the quality of your mind in this very moment.**

**6. The Core Buddhist Method: Look and Adjust**
The key practical method is encapsulated in a saying from Dhammarato's teacher, Buddhadasa: **"If at first you don't succeed, look at what you're doing."** This is the essence of the path‚Äî**mindfulness as course-correction**. Instead of stubbornly repeating failed patterns (like arguing over a gym machine), you observe the result (suffering), see the error (ownership, childish anger), and change your approach (walk away, practice "NMB").

**In essence,** this talk is a practical guide to using life's irritations to wake up. It teaches:
*   See through childish impulses of ownership and conflict.
*   Adopt the "Not My Business" attitude to shed mental burdens.
*   Cultivate inner friendship through present-moment awareness.
*   Value direct experience over speculative beliefs.
*   Use the "look and adjust" method to break habitual suffering and choose peace, right here and now.
