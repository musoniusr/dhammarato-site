2.  **The Body as a "Peripheral Field":** Extending the vision metaphor, the speaker says the body is "also like that." When we focus mentally on one body part, the rest becomes "peripheral." The goal is to soften that focus to "allow the peripheral to be much larger and take in data."

3.  **Hands as Eyes / "Seeing with Hands":** This is a central metaphor for developing tactile awareness.
    *   The speaker says a skilled car mechanic can **"see with their hands"** when working in a tight space they can't visually access.
    *   Developing proprioception (the sense of your body in space) means your hands can become like eyes. **"Proprioceptive system is what puts our body in the position... to be able to stand and not fall."**
    *   The goal is to **"let the body's knowledge of the environment expand so that you know the touch... That proprioceptive actually will make eyes out of your hands."**

4.  **Boundary of the Self:** The speaker uses the metaphor of a **"boundary"** between self and environment.
    *   A defensive teenager has **"a very definite boundary of who she thinks she is based upon where the skin is."**
    *   A mystic or mindful person has a **"much looser"** boundary, **"expanding his awareness and bringing it out."**

5.  **Concentration vs. Samadhi:** The speaker distinguishes between two types of mental focus using a spatial metaphor.
    *   **Concentration** is described as **"narrowing our focus and bringing it down to a tight point,"** like excluding sensory input.
    *   **Samadhi** is described as being **"all encompassed, we're bringing everything in."**

### **Stories & Anecdotes**

1.  **The Bird in the Room:** The speaker tells a story of a bird flying through their open-house. **"I was the only one that saw the bird because everybody else was focused and didn't see it."** This story illustrates the practical benefit of broad, peripheral awareness versus narrow focus.

2.  **The City Robber & Subconscious Awareness:** The story of a potential robber approaching someone from behind uses the **"hair on the back of their neck"** standing up as a signal. This illustrates how the body perceives danger subconsciously, and how thieves avoid people who show this subtle, aware reaction. It's compared to a dog's hackles raising.

3.  **Training with Achan Po:** The speaker shares personal training stories:
    *   Achan Po would **"intentionally come up quietly behind me and whisper in my ear"** until the speaker became so aware he could no longer be snuck up on.
    *   Achan Po would **"stand in the yard of my kuti... without making a sound, just to see how long it took me to come to my senses."** These stories frame awareness as a trainable skill for being "alert and awake."

4.  **The Dishwasher Startled:** At Wat Suwan Mok, a woman washing dishes was **"really spooked out and freaked out"** when she suddenly noticed the speaker standing beside her. This story warns against the hyper-focus that makes one oblivious to their environment and serves as a contrast to the desired state of open awareness.

5.  **The "Flying" Monk Legend:** The speaker explains the mythical idea that some monks can fly or float. They demystify it: it comes from the quiet, graceful movement developed through walking meditation. When someone who is **"not paying attention"** is approached by such a person, it can seem like they **"floated into the room."** This story links physical practice (walking meditation) to perceptual outcomes.

6.  **The First Baseman:** The story of a baseball first baseman catching a ball without conscious thought of hand placement is used to explain **proprioception**‚Äîa subconscious skill that can be brought into conscious awareness through mindfulness.

7.  **Lucky the Dog:** The speaker's dog, Lucky, is used as a recurring example:
    *   She instinctively comes for petting when the speaker's hands are free, illustrating the natural desire for pleasant touch and energy exchange.
    *   The speaker compares his own trained awareness to Lucky's guard dog instincts, creating a light **"happy competition"** to highlight how mindfulness can make one more aware of their surroundings than even a watchful animal.

These metaphors and stories work together to make abstract concepts like proprioception, expanded awareness, and somatic mindfulness tangible, memorable, and practical.
