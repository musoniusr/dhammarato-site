### Summary

This Dhamma talk, led by Dhammarato, addresses a common meditation question: **"How do I stop daydreaming?"** The core teaching is that this is the wrong question, and trying to *stop* the mind is impossible and counterproductive.

Here is a summary of the key points:

### **1. The Wrong Approach: Trying to "Stop" the Mind**
*   The mind has no brakes, like a large ship or a rowboat. You cannot forcibly halt its flow of thoughts.
*   Attempting to stop daydreams or hating them is itself an **unwholesome thought** (a hindrance like aversion or ill-will). It creates a cycle of frustration: "Oh no, here they come again... I can't do this... poor me."

### **2. The Right Approach: Steering and Gladdening the Mind**
*   Instead of stopping, **skillfully steer** the mind away from danger, just as ships turn to avoid collision.
*   The crucial shift is in **attitude**. When you notice daydreaming, **congratulate yourself**: "Aha! I caught it. I see you, Mara (the tempter)."
*   This moment of recognition is a victory‚Äîit is **Sati (mindfulness)** arising. The congratulatory thought is a **wholesome, present-moment thought** that replaces the unwholesome daydream.

### **3. Training the Mind Like a Puppy or a Wild Horse**
*   **Don't punish the untrained mind.** Scolding yourself for daydreaming is like beating a puppy for not obeying a command it was never taught.
*   **Reward good behavior.** Every time you notice distraction, reward the mind with gladness and praise. This builds confidence and wholesome habits.
*   **Establish boundaries.** You can't stop a wild horse, but you can corral it. Gradually limit the mind's range:
    *   From **any thought at all** (no boundaries).
    *   To **only wholesome thoughts** (the corral).
    *   To thoughts **only about the present place and moment**.
    *   Eventually, to a **single mantra or the breath**.
*   Each time the mind crosses a boundary and you gently bring it back with joy, you strengthen your control.

### **4. Practical Method: Anapanasati (Mindfulness of Breathing)**
*   Use the breath as the primary training ground. The act of **remembering** to take a long, deep, enjoyable breath is itself mindfulness (Sati).
*   By controlling the breath in a relaxed, pleasurable way, you are directly training the mind in control and focus.
*   When the mind wanders from the breath, apply the formula: **"Gotcha. Never mind. Okay, let's start again with a wholesome breath."**

### **5. The Essential Attitude Shift**
*   Your entire practice depends on changing from **being unhappy *with* the mind** for its wanderings to **being happy *that you can see*** its wanderings.
*   This **gladdening of the mind** is a formal step in Anapanasati and is the key to building a joyful, sustainable practice.
*   The common mistake‚Äîfeeling like a failure for daydreaming‚Äîcauses many to quit. The correct path is to **talk yourself into feeling good** about your growing awareness, breaking the habit of talking yourself into feeling bad.

**In essence:** Stop trying to stop the daydreams. Start celebrating the moment you notice them. Use that moment of awareness to gently and joyfully steer the mind back to a wholesome object like the breath. Train the mind with rewards and boundaries, not with force and criticism. This transforms meditation from a battle into a skillful and joyful training.
