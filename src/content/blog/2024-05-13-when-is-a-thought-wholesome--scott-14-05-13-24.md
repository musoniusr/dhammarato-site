### Summary

This Dhamma talk, led by Dhammarato (Speaker A) with Scott (Speaker B) and other Sangha friends, focuses on **cultivating wholesome thoughts and right samadhi (unification of mind) in everyday life.** Here is a summary of the key points:

### **Core Theme: Wholesome vs. Unwholesome Thoughts**
*   **Unwholesome Thoughts:** Arise from a sense of lack, fear, or victimhood. Examples include thoughts of revenge, competition, complaining, "I'm not okay," or "things could get better."
*   **Wholesome Thoughts:** Arise from acceptance and contentment with the present moment. Examples include "everything's okay," "this is nice," or "there's no place to go, nothing to do."
*   **The "Middle Ground":** Many thoughts are neutral. The key is to **investigate them with a positive, factual attitude** (e.g., "This is broken, but I can fix it or find someone who can") rather than a negative, reactive one (e.g., "Oh no, it's broken and I'm stuck").

### **Practical Method: The Feedback Loop**
*   A core practice is to **congratulate yourself** each time you spot an unwholesome thought. This creates a positive, wholesome feedback loop that reinforces mindfulness.

### **Right Samadhi: Unifying the Mind**
*   Dhammarato explains **right samadhi** as a mind unified in reality, free from the conflict between three internal voices (borrowed from Freud/Transactional Analysis):
    1.  **The Parent:** How things "should" be (ideals, rules).
    2.  **The Child:** How we "want" things to be (desires, cravings).
    3.  **The Adult:** How things actually *are* (reality).
*   A mind in samadhi spends most of its time in the **"Adult" state**‚Äîaccepting reality as it is‚Äîwithout being disturbed by the demanding "shoulds" of the Parent or the greedy "wants" of the Child.
*   **Example:** One can be fat, have diabetes, and *be okay with that reality* in the present moment. From that peaceful, accepting space, wise action (like putting down unhealthy food) arises naturally, without internal struggle or self-judgment.

### **Applications to Daily Life**
*   **Sports & Performance:** The mindset of "I must score" creates anxiety and self-sabotage. The wholesome mindset is "I'm okay right now; I can practice/play from here."
*   **Dealing with Criticism & Anger:**
    *   **Anger** stems from **fear**‚Äîoften a primal fear of social rejection or loss of status ("being outside the herd").
    *   When criticized, understand: **People are not angry at *you*; they are angry at their *mental idea* of you** (e.g., "the referee," a rival, a uniform). They don't know the real you.
    *   From this perspective, you can remain in a wholesome state, feel compassion for their misunderstanding, and let criticism roll off "like water off a duck's back."
*   **Finding Spiritual Community:** Authentic Dhamma (understanding reality) can be found anywhere, not just in formal Buddhism. It's present in anyone investigating "the way things actually are," including certain Christian mystics who focus on direct experience of divine presence/paradise in the here and now, as opposed to dogmatic beliefs.

### **Overall Message**
The path is about **continuously returning to a wholesome, contented, and realistic perspective on the present moment.** By congratulating yourself for spotting unwholesome thoughts, you train the mind to dwell in acceptance. From this unified, peaceful state (samadhi), wise and compassionate action flows naturally, and you see that you are already "in the middle of the herd"‚Äîsecure and at home in reality.
