### Summary

This Dhamma talk between Dhammarato (Speaker B) and a student (Speaker A) centers on a profound shift in perception the student is experiencing during walking meditation. Here is a summary of the key themes and insights:

### **Core Insight: From "Noun-Based" to "Verb-Based" Experience**
The student describes a shift where sensations (like wind on the skin) are no longer experienced as "**me** feeling the wind." Instead, there is just the raw experience‚Äî"the wind feeling itself" or "the sensation experiencing itself." This is a move away from a self-centered (*atta*) view.

Dhammarato frames this as a shift from a **noun-based** reality (solid, separate things like "me" and "wind") to a **verb-based** reality (a process of "blowing," "feeling," "walking"). He references the famous line, "There is walking, but no walker," to illustrate this non-self (*anatta*) perspective.

### **Key Discussion Points**

1.  **Language as an Approximation:** Our language, built around pronouns and nouns, forces us to speak "incorrectly" about direct experience. Descriptions and concepts always come *after* the experience and can never capture it fully. The goal is to be satisfied with "good enough" approximations without getting lost in conceptualizing.

2.  **The Problem of the "Experiencer":** The central issue in meditation isn't the experience itself, but our tendency to process it through the lens of a self. Students often ask, "What does this experience mean **for me**? Am I enlightened now?" This very question reinforces the illusion of the experiencer and leads to *dukkha* (suffering).

3.  **Jhana Reinterpreted:** Dhammarato challenges a common Western Buddhist view of *jhanas* as advanced states to be "attained" by a self. Instead, he presents them (particularly the fourth *jhana* and the formless realms) as **ways of looking** that prioritize receiving raw sensory data over processing it.
    *   The goal is to simplify the mind's "program" so it can receive more data with less interpretive processing.
    *   Experiences like "infinite space" are better understood as "boundarylessness"‚Äîa direct experience where the felt boundary between body and environment dissolves, not a metaphysical infinite realm.

4.  **The Computer Analogy: Sampling Rate vs. Processing:**
    *   **Low Sampling Rate / High Processing:** Our normal, "fast thinking" mode. We take in little sensory data ("low sampling") and spend excessive time processing it through past conditioning, narratives, and self-reference. This leads to outdated conclusions, confusion, and solidification of reality.
    *   **High Sampling Rate / Low Processing:** The meditative goal. To receive a "flood" of present-moment sensory data ("high sampling") with minimal conceptual processing. This means allowing oneself to be "happily ignorant," dropping the need for immediate answers, and just experiencing the "flickering" sensations as they are.

5.  **Connection to Dependent Origination (*Paticcasamuppada*):** This process maps the chain from sensory contact (*phassa*) to self-formation and *dukkha*. By stopping the compulsive processing that creates "the experiencer," we can halt this chain at the level of bare sensation.

### **Conclusion: The Practical Path**
The talk concludes that the essence of this advanced practice is simple: **"Let's just get data and stop doing so much processing of it."** The student's walking meditation is a direct application‚Äîexperiencing sights, sounds, and bodily sensations as a seamless, boundaryless field of changing phenomena without a central owner.

The ultimate point is that this direct, verb-based experiencing leads to freedom, while the noun-based processing of experiences through the self is what creates unnecessary complexity and suffering.
