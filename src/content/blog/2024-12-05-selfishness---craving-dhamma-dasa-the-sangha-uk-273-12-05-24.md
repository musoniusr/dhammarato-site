### Summary

Based on this Dhamma talk, here is a summary of the key teachings and advice given by Dhammarato (Speaker B) to a practitioner (Speaker A, likely named Pedro) who has been struggling:

**Core Problem:** The practitioner has been isolating himself from the Sangha (spiritual community) for about a year and a half to "go it alone." **Dhammarato's Diagnosis & Core Teachings:**

1.  **The Issue is Mental, Not Physical:** The practitioner describes his "shutdown" as physical, but Dhammarato corrects him‚Äîit's a mental event, a choice to stop watching the mind.
2.  **"Out of Control" is an Unwholesome Thought:** The feeling that thoughts are "out of control" is itself an unwholesome, disempowering thought. It reinforces a victim mentality.
3.  **There's Nothing "Deep" to Uncover:** The practitioner speculates he's uncovering deep issues. Dhammarato counters that the patterns are surface-level and repetitive, not mysterious profundities needing excavation.
4.  **The Error is in the Timing & Frequency of Practice:** The main mistake is allowing unwholesome thought patterns to run for **days** instead of intercepting them within **seconds or minutes**. The practice has become too infrequent.

**Prescribed Solutions & Advice:**

1.  **Practice "Often," Not "Deep":** Shift the focus from occasional, intense practice to frequent, momentary check-ins throughout the day.
2.  **Use "Remembering to Look":** The core technique is to **remember to look at your thoughts** as they arise to evaluate if they are wholesome or unwholesome.
3.  **Implement Frequent Reminders:** Set an alarm to go off **once every hour**. When it rings, use that moment to "remember to look" and consciously cultivate wholesome thoughts.
4.  **Cultivate "Winner's Thoughts":** Actively replace unwholesome thoughts (e.g., "This is hard," "I can't control this") with empowering, wholesome ones (e.g., "I can do this," "I can be kind," "I am not a victim of these thoughts").
