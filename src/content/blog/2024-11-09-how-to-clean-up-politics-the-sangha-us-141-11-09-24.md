### Summary

This Dhamma talk, led by Dhammarato (Speaker A), uses a participant's (Eric/Speaker B) acute anxiety following a U.S. presidential election as a practical lesson in core Buddhist principles. Here is a summary of the key points:

**Core Teaching: Suffering is Optional and Self-Created**
Dhammarato redirects the conversation from politics to Dhamma (the Buddha's teachings). The central theme is that Eric's fear and suffering are not caused by external events (e.g., Donald Trump's election), but by his own thoughts about those events. The pain is "between your ears."

**Main Arguments and Instructions:**

1.  **Uncertainty of the Future:** Dhammarato repeatedly emphasizes that we cannot know the future. Eric's fears are based on predictions, rumors, and promises (which, like marriage vows, are often broken), not on present reality.
2.  **The Role of Media and Fear:** The talk identifies media (news, social media) as a business that sells "lies, rumors, and most importantly, fear." By consuming it, Eric has "bought a potload" of fear.
3.  **Power of the Present Moment:** The practical instruction is to return to the present. Whenever a fearful thought arises, one should check: "Am I safe right now?" Since, in the immediate moment, most are physically safe, they can affirm, "I'm okay now. Not a problem in the world."
4.  **Letting Go and Non-Engagement:** Dhammarato advises mental and physical disengagement. Since Eric's fear doesn't help the millions who are suffering, it is "optional." He should "throw Trump out of his mind" just as he would any unwholesome thought. The instruction extends to turning off news sources.
5.  **The "Not Yet" Practice:** A key technique offered is to replace catastrophic thinking with the phrase "not yet." (e.g., "There has been no nuclear war *yet*"). This acknowledges uncertainty while grounding oneself in the current, still-okay reality.
6.  **The Real Enemy is Internal:** The talk concludes that the "Donald Trump" causing Eric's suffering is not the external person, but the internal mental image and narrative Eric has created and fixated upon. "You're the one who is coming after you."

**Metaphors and Examples:**
*   **The School Paddling:** Dhammarato shares a childhood story where the fear of punishment was worse than the punishment itself, illustrating how anticipated suffering is often greater than actual suffering.

**Conclusion:**
The talk is a direct application of the **Four Noble Truths**: Eric's suffering (*dukkha*) is caused by his craving and clinging to fearful narratives about the future. The cessation (*nirodha*) of that suffering is possible by letting go of those thoughts and abiding in the present.
