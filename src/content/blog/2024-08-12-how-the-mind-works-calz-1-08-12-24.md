2.  **Putting Makeup on a Pig:** Used by Speaker A to criticize "toxic positivity." It's superficial‚Äîit might look nicer (the makeup), but the underlying reality (the pig) remains unchanged. This contrasts with the genuine peace found in accepting truth.
3.  **The Teflon Mind / Oiled Rock:** A metaphor championed by Dhammarato (referenced by Speaker A). The mind should be like a Teflon pan or an oiled stone, where thoughts, judgments, and worries "slip right off" without sticking.
4.  **Making Your Own Ham Sandwich:** The story told by Speaker F illustrates the victim mentality. A man complains daily about his ham sandwich, only to reveal he makes them himself. The metaphor: we often create our own unsatisfactory mental states and then blame external circumstances.
5.  **Stepping Around Geese Shit:** Used by Speaker A to describe the practice. You see something unpleasant (a negative thought, a judgment), and you simply, gently step around it instead of dwelling in it or fighting it.
6.  **Sucking / Things to Suck On:** Speaker F uses this metaphor to explain why we need teachings. "Life sucks because you're sucking" (you're wanting). The Buddha gives us wholesome things (teachings, practices) "to suck on" until we learn to stop the habitual "sucking" or craving altogether.
7.  **The Mind Fit for Work:** Referenced by Speaker F (from Buddhadasa). A calm, happy, and satisfied mind is "fit for work"‚Äîmeaning it's clear and stable enough to effectively investigate deeper truths and uproot suffering.
8.  **Waiting for a Bus at the Wrong Stop:** Speaker F uses this to describe ineffective meditation. Sitting and hoping to feel better is like waiting for a bus (peace) at the wrong stop (from a place of wanting and dissatisfaction).

**Stories & References:**

1.  **Bodhidharma:** Speaker A and C discuss the legendary Zen master. They mention the (likely apocryphal) story of his disciple cutting off his own arm to prove dedication. Bodhidharma is used as an example of the direct teaching to "discover your true nature," which is equated with peace and letting go.
2.  **The Flood Zone / Wildfire Homestead:** Speaker F uses this analogy to explain skillful vs. unskillful contemplation. Skillful contemplation is like checking a property for environmental risks (e.g., flood zones, wildfires)‚Äîit's a clear-eyed assessment of where suffering might arise. Unskillful contemplation is an anxious proliferation of "what-ifs" driven by craving.
3.  **Mara:** Personified as the tempter or deceiver. Speaker A advises catching negative patterns by saying, "I see you, Mara, you little bastard," and then walking around them, just as one would avoid literal excrement.

**Core Narrative Through-Line:**
The conversation itself follows a **story arc from problem to solution**:
*   **Problem:** The habit of judgment and victimhood (the arrows, the ham sandwich).
*   **False Solution:** Toxic positivity or passive acceptance (makeup on a pig).
*   **True Solution:** Cultivating a satisfied mind ("fit for work") that sees the truth of the present moment. From this place, one can let things go, developing a "Teflon mind" where sufferings slip away, and one simply "steps around" mental geese shit. The ultimate goal is to stop "sucking" on craving altogether and be okay with reality as it is.
