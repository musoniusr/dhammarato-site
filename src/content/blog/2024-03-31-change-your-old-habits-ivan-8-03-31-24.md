### Summary

Based on the dialogue, here is a summary of the core Dhamma talk between Dhammarato (Speaker B) and a practitioner (Speaker A):

**Core Teaching: Stop "Figuring It Out" and Realize "Everything is Already Okay"**

The central theme is a shift from problem-solving mode to a mode of relaxed, non-judgmental observation and self-reassurance. The practitioner is caught in cycles of self-doubt, over-analysis, and the habitual feeling that something is wrong and needs to be fixed.

**Key Points from Dhammarato's Guidance:**

1.  **You Don't Need to "Figure It Out":** The attempt to intellectually analyze or "fix" your negative thoughts and emotions is itself the problem. It's compared to picking up dog waste and trying to make food from itâ€”the solution is to simply step aside.
2.  **Acknowledge and Dis-identify:** Instead of engaging, use simple, observational phrases like: *"Oh, there it is again," "I see that stuff,"* or *"Hello, Mara."* This creates space between you and the habitual thought pattern.
3.  **Replace the Narrative with Truth:** Actively counter the old, negative mental habits with truthful, positive phrases. Repeat them like a mantra:
    *   *"Everything is okay."*
    *   *"I can handle this."*
    *   *"No worries, no problems."*
    *   *"I don't have to figure it out."*
    The point is that these statements are *true*â€”the perceived problems are self-created mental habits.
4.  **Congratulate Yourself on Awareness:** The moment you see the old habit arising is a victory. Be happy you can see it; you don't then need to fix what you see. This changes your relationship to the difficulty.
5.  **Practice Being Okay:** The goal isn't to achieve a future state of okay-ness through struggle. It's to **practice relaxing into the truth that you are already okay in this moment**, despite the passing thoughts and feelings.
6.  **Have Fun as an Observer:** Learn to find relief and even humor in watching your own mental habits. *"Wow, I'm really champion at tensing up!"* This lightens the struggle and reinforces the role of the observer.

**The Practitioner's Struggles (which Dhammarato addresses):**
*   Feeling meditation should be used to "deal with" emotions, creating more striving.
*   Strong aversion (*"I don't want to feel this"*) towards old, painful memories and feelings.
*   Feeling internal rebellion even when trying to be kind and self-caring.
*   A deep-seated fear that "everything is about to go wrong," which fuels the need to figure things out.
*   Physical tension that arises alongside the old mental patterns.

**Conclusion:** The talk is a practical guide to breaking the cycle of self-critical suffering. The path is to **stop the struggle**, **see thoughts as mere old habits**, and **repeatedly relax into the present-moment truth that nothing is fundamentally wrong.** The practice is the gentle, persistent return to the phrases and attitude of "Everything is all right."
