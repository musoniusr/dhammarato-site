### Summary

This Dhamma talk centers on a practitioner (Speaker A) describing a breakthrough in meditation where they experienced a still, "empty" mind with thoughts distant and fleeting, like slow-moving trains or passing clouds. Dhammarato (Speaker B) guides the discussion, emphasizing correct practice and clarifying core concepts. Here is a summary of the key points:

### **Core Themes of the Talk**

1.  **The Importance of First Jhana:** Dhammarato repeatedly stresses that the primary goal of practice is to master the **First Jhana**, not to chase higher states. The First Jhana is defined as a sustained state of **applied and sustained wholesome thought**, characterized by feelings of **safety, security, comfort, satisfaction, and success (Sukkha)**, which then naturally give rise to **joy (Piti)**. It's the foundational "work" of meditation.

2.  **"Jacking Up" the Mind:** A central analogy is using wholesome, nurturing thoughts like a car jack to lift the mind into a joyful, concentrated state. This involves continuously and intentionally applying thoughts like "I am safe," "This is good," "I am satisfied," until the positive feeling becomes self-sustaining. This process *is* the cultivation of First Jhana.

3.  **Right Attitude & Non-Judgment:** The practitioner is warned against comparing current meditative states to past "peak" experiences (like the "emptiness" they described). Judging the present as "not as good" creates hindrances. The correct attitude is to find the wholesome in the present moment and nurture it: "This is good enough right now."

4.  **Clarification of the "Empty" Mind:** Dhammarato clarifies that the mind doesn't fully "shut off" (which would be death), but rather, through stability, perception speeds up. Thoughts appear distant and slow, like watching a train from far away or using a high-speed cameraâ€”this is a sign of increasing mindfulness and clarity, not a blank void.

5.  **Practical Foundations:** The path to this concentrated state requires creating the right conditions:
    *   A **physically safe, secure, and comfortable** environment (even using a chair if needed).
    *   **Relaxing the body** completely by investigating and releasing tension.
    *   Using **nurturing self-talk** to convince the mind and body of this safety and satisfaction.

6.  **Purpose of a Unified Mind:** Once the mind is unified and "fit for work" (in First Jhana), it is freed from hindrances and capable of true insight. The work then is to **apply and sustain that wholesome state itself**. Higher jhanas (like taking joy itself as the object) arise naturally from this solid foundation, but they are not the primary aim.

7.  **Lay Practice is Valid:** In the final part of the talk, Dhammarato encourages the practitioner, noting that full awakening is possible as a layperson. One does not need to ordain as a monk, but should seek noble friendship (like the online Sanghas mentioned) and correct instruction.

### **Conclusion**
The talk is a practical guide steering a practitioner away from chasing special "empty" states and toward the diligent, nurturing work of building **First Jhana**. The key is the intentional cultivation of wholesome thoughts and feelings in the present moment, using them as a tool to unify the mind and make it "fit for work." The experience of stillness and distant thoughts is framed not as a goal, but as a confirming sign of correct, sustained practice.
